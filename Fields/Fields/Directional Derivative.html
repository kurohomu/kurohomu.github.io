Directional Derivative

The [[Fields/Fields/Gradient | gradient]] is a vector that describe the rapid decreasing directional of the field. However, we consider all direction of field. Directional derivative is the way to find the derivative of particular direction.

#Definition
If $f(x, y, z)$ has continuous partial derivatives at the point $P_{0}\left(x_{0}, y_{0}, z_{0}\right)$ and $\hat{\mathbf{u}}$ is a unit vector, then the derivative of $f$ at $P_{0}$ in the direction of $\hat{\mathbf{u}}$ is the number$$
\left.\left(D_{\hat{\mathbf{u}}} f\right)\right|{P{0}}=\left.(\nabla f)\right|{P{0}} \cdot \hat{\mathbf{u}}$$
which is the scalar product of the gradient of $f$ at the point $P_{0}$ and the unit vector $\hat{\mathbf{u}}$.

  • Element: Field, gradient, unit vector, point
  • The point we interest must has continuous partial derivatives
  • Pay attention to the notation

It is a general way to find the change rate of field. The gradient is only a special case of this.

Proof.

We seek the derivative of directional. Hence, a fix point and a directional point would be need.

  1. Set $P_0, P, u, s$
  2. Find representation of line $L$ with $s$
  3. Use chain rule to solve derivative

    Property

    Since the directional derivative is the dot product, many properties following:

#Property

  1. If $\nabla f\left(x_{0}, y_{0}, z_{0}\right)=\mathbf{0}$, then all the directional derivatives of $f$ at the point $\left(x_{0}, y_{0}, z_{0}\right)$ are zero.

Direct from formula. The case of all zero. It mean that the derivatives never change whatever the direction change.

  1. If $\nabla f\left(x_{0}, y_{0}, z_{0}\right) \neq \mathbf{0}$, then as the direction of $\hat{\mathbf{u}}$ changes, the directional derivative $\nabla f\left(x_{0}, y_{0}, z_{0}\right) \cdot \hat{\mathbf{u}}$ changes. Of all the possible directional derivatives of $f$ at $P_{0}$, it has its maximum value when $\hat{\mathbf{u}}$ is in the direction of $\nabla f\left(x_{0}, y_{0}, z_{0}\right)$, i.e. when $\theta=0$, meaning $\cos \theta=1$. This is consequence of the fact that the maximum value of $\cos \theta$ is 1.
    When $\theta=0$, the value of that directional derivative is given by$$\left.\left(D_{\hat{\mathrm{u}}} f\right)\right|{\left(x{0}, y_{0}, z_{0}\right)}=\left|\nabla f\left(x_{0}, y_{0}, z_{0}\right)\right| \cos (0)=\left|\nabla f\left(x_{0}, y_{0}, z_{0}\right)\right|$$
    We say that $f$ increases most rapidly in the direction of $\nabla f$.

Relate with gradient: the direction of maximum is the gradient Since the dot product: $cos\theta$ have the maximum 1 and unit vector have the normal 1. Hence the directional derivative is less than gradient.

  1. If $\nabla f\left(x_{0}, y_{0}, z_{0}\right) \neq \mathbf{0}$ and $\left.\nabla f\right|{P{0}}$ is in the direction of $\hat{\mathbf{u}}$, then$$
    \hat{\mathbf{u}}=\frac{\nabla f\left(x_{0}, y_{0}, z_{0}\right)}{\left|\nabla f\left(x_{0}, y_{0}, z_{0}\right)\right|}$$Therefore$$
    \begin{aligned}
    \left.\left(D_{\hat{\mathbf{u}}} f\right)\right|{\left(x{0}, y_{0}, z_{0}\right)} &=\frac{\nabla f\left(x_{0}, y_{0}, z_{0}\right)}{\left|\nabla f\left(x_{0}, y_{0}, z_{0}\right)\right|} \cdot \nabla f\left(x_{0}, y_{0}, z_{0}\right) \
    &=\frac{\left|\nabla f\left(x_{0}, y_{0}, z_{0}\right)\right|^{2}}{\left|\nabla f\left(x_{0}, y_{0}, z_{0}\right)\right|}=\left|\nabla f\left(x_{0}, y_{0}, z_{0}\right)\right|
    \end{aligned}$$

Prove that the directional derivative with direction gradient is the normal of gradient without using the geometric property of dot product.

  1. If $\nabla f\left(x_{0}, y_{0}, z_{0}\right) \neq \mathbf{0}$, then among all the possible directional derivatives of $f$ at the point $\left(x_{0}, y_{0}, z_{0}\right)$, the derivative in the direction opposite to that of $\nabla f\left(x_{0}, y_{0}, z_{0}\right)$ (i.e., when $\cos \theta=-1$, meaning $\theta=\pi$ ) has the smallest value. The value of the directional derivative is$$\left.\left(D_{\hat{\mathbf{u}}} f\right)\right|{\left(x{0}, y_{0}, z_{0}\right)}=\left|\nabla f\left(x_{0}, y_{0}, z_{0}\right)\right||\hat{\mathbf{u}}| \cos (\pi)=-\left|\nabla f\left(x_{0}, y_{0}, z_{0}\right)\right|$$

Essential of gradient decent. That mean the place that direction live is a plain.

  1. Any direction $\hat{\mathbf{u}}$ which is normal (perpendicular) to $\left.(\nabla f)\right|{P{0}}$ corresponds to zero change in $f$, i.e., a zero directional derivative, because$$\left.\left(D_{\hat{\mathbf{u}}} f\right)\right|{P{0}}=\left|\nabla f\left(x_{0}, y_{0}, z_{0}\right)\right||\hat{\mathbf{u}}| \cos \left(\frac{\pi}{2}\right)=0$$

similar to above.

Those property illustrate a view to directional derivative. For a point, it lives in a plain, and the value determinate by the point $cos\theta$.

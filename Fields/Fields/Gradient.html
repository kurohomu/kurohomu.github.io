Gradient

If $G(x, y, z)$ is a differentiable [[Fields/Fields/Scalar and Vector Field#Scalar Field| scalar field]], then we define the gradient of $G$ (in Cartesian coordinates), written $\nabla G$ (or grad $G$ ) by
$$
\nabla G=\frac{\partial G}{\partial x} \hat{\mathbf{i}}+\frac{\partial G}{\partial y} \hat{\mathbf{j}}+\frac{\partial G}{\partial z} \hat{\mathbf{k}}
$$
The gradient is an operator, which input a differentiable scalar field, output a vector.

What is the mean of this operator and output? We have following theorem to illustrate the intuitive comprehension.

#Theorem
If scalar field $G$ is differentiable at $\left(x_{0}, y_{0}, z_{0}\right)$ and $\nabla G\left(x_{0}, y_{0}, z_{0}\right) \neq \mathbf{0}$, then $\nabla G\left(x_{0}, y_{0}, z_{0}\right)$ is normal (perpendicular) to the [[Fields/Fields/Scalar and Vector Field#Level Surface | level surface]] of $G(x, y, z)$ through $\left(x_{0}, y_{0}, z_{0}\right)$

^a155c4

  • The differential condition derive from the definition of the gradient.
  • When gradient equal 0, the level surface become a point. ($\mathbf{0}$ normal to any vector.)
  • In general, level surface is the field, gradient is its derivative.
Proof.

*Idea of small change express by Taylor Expansion. *

Take the point $P_{0}^{\prime}$ on $G(x, y, z)=k$ near the point $P_{0}$
Then $G\left(P_{0}^{\prime}\right)-G\left(P_{0}\right)=k-k=0$
Now
$$
G\left(P_{0}^{\prime}\right)-G\left(P_{0}\right)=G\left(x_{0}+\delta x, y_{0}+\delta y, z_{0}+\delta z\right)-G\left(x_{0}, y_{0}, z_{0}\right)
$$
By Taylor series expansion (in several variables) this gives
$$
\begin{aligned}
G\left(P_{0}^{\prime}\right)-G\left(P_{0}\right) &=\left.\frac{\partial G}{\partial x}\right|{P{0}} \delta x+\left.\frac{\partial G}{\partial y}\right|{P{0}} \delta y+\left.\frac{\partial G}{\partial z}\right|{P{0}} \delta z+\text { smaller terms } \
&=(\delta x, \delta y, \delta z) \cdot\left(\frac{\partial G}{\partial x}, \frac{\partial G}{\partial y}, \frac{\partial G}{\partial z}\right){P{0}} \
&=P_{0} P_{0}^{\prime} \cdot \nabla G\left(x_{0}, y_{0}, z_{0}\right)
\end{aligned}
$$
Therefore we have shown $P_{0} \vec{P}{0}^{\prime} \cdot \nabla G\left(x{0}, y_{0}, z_{0}\right)=0$ which implies the vector $P_{0} \vec{P}{0}^{\prime}$ is perpendicular to $\nabla G\left(x{0}, y_{0}, z_{0}\right)$

vector operator

Sometimes it is convince to consider the vector operator. The vector operator $\nabla$ is defined to be in Cartesian coordinates
$$
\nabla=\hat{\mathbf{i}} \frac{\partial}{\partial x}+\hat{\mathbf{j}} \frac{\partial}{\partial y}+\hat{\mathbf{k}} \frac{\partial}{\partial z}=\left(\frac{\partial}{\partial x}, \frac{\partial}{\partial y}, \frac{\partial}{\partial z}\right)
$$
When this acts on a scalar field, say $G(x, y, z)$, it produces the vector $\nabla G(x, y, z)$.

A natural extension is for the vector operator, as defined above, to act on vector fields such as
$$
\mathbf{F}(x, y, z)=F_{x}(x, y, z) \hat{\mathbf{i}}+F_{y}(x, y, z) \hat{\mathbf{j}}+F_{z}(x, y, z) \hat{\mathbf{k}}
$$
If we regard $\nabla$ as a vector (even though strictly speaking it is a vector operator), there are two possible types of product for the combination of two vectors. Consider the vectors $\mathbf{a}$ and $\mathbf{b}$. Then we have

  1. scalar or dot product between two vectors, $\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b}$;
  2. vector or cross product between two vectors, $\mathbf{a} \wedge \mathbf{b}$ or $\mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{b}$

#property
(i) $\nabla\left(G_{1}+G_{2}\right)=\nabla G_{1}+\nabla G_{2}$
(ii) $\nabla\left(G_{1} G_{2}\right)=G_{1} \nabla G_{2}+G_{2} \nabla G_{1}$

Summary

The gradient of a scalar field G is a vector field, with a direction which is normal to the level surfaces of the [[Fields/Fields/Scalar and Vector Field#Scalar Field| scalar field]], pointing in the direction of increasing $G$ with a magnitude equal to the rate of change of $G$ in this direction

Find normal vector

Given arbitrary surface(level surface of field), find the normal vector of particular point.

We use the [[#^a155c4 | theorem]]. The normal vector is just the gradient of level surface with differential and not zero condition. This theorem is apply to level surface. Hence, we should declare that the surface we use is the level surface of some field.

Pay attention to transit to unit vector according to the problem.

Step

  2. Calculate the gradient of field

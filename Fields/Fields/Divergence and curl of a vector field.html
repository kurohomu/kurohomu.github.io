Divergence and curl of a vector field

The [[Fields/Fields/Gradient|gradient]] operation with [[Fields/Fields/Scalar and Vector Field#Vector Field|vector field]]

Divergence of a vector field

The scalar product between $\nabla$ and a vector field $\mathbf{F}$, is $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F}$.
This is called the divergence of the vector field $\mathbf{F}$ (often written as div $\mathbf{F}$ ) and in Cartesian coordinates is explicitly given by
$$
\operatorname{div} \mathbf{F}=\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F}=\left(\hat{\mathbf{i}} \frac{\partial}{\partial x}+\hat{\mathbf{j}} \frac{\partial}{\partial y}+\hat{\mathbf{k}} \frac{\partial}{\partial z}\right) \cdot\left(F_{x} \hat{\mathbf{i}}+F_{y} \hat{\mathbf{j}}+F_{z} \hat{\mathbf{k}}\right)=\frac{\partial F_{x}}{\partial x}+\frac{\partial F_{y}}{\partial y}+\frac{\partial F_{z}}{\partial z}
$$
The divergence of a vector field is a [[Fields/Fields/Scalar and Vector Field#Scalar Field | scalar field]].

Please note, as $\nabla$ is a vector operator and not a vector, then $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F} \neq \mathbf{F} \cdot \nabla$.

irrotational

A vector field with zero curl is said to be conservative. We also say that such a field is irrotational.

Curl of a vector field

The vector product between $\nabla$ and a vector field $\mathbf{F}$, is $\nabla \wedge \mathbf{F}$ or $\nabla \times \mathbf{F}$.
This is called the curl of the vector field $\mathbf{F}$ (often written as curl $\mathbf{F}$ ) and in Cartesian coordinates is explicitly given by
$$
\begin{aligned}
\operatorname{curl} \mathbf{F} &=\nabla \times \mathbf{F} \
&=\left(\hat{\mathbf{i}} \frac{\partial}{\partial x}+\hat{\mathbf{j}} \frac{\partial}{\partial y}+\hat{\mathbf{k}} \frac{\partial}{\partial z}\right) \times\left(F_{x} \hat{\mathbf{i}}+F_{y} \hat{\mathbf{j}}+F_{z} \hat{\mathbf{k}}\right) \
&=\left|\begin{array}{ccc}
\hat{\mathbf{i}} & \hat{\mathbf{j}} & \hat{\mathbf{k}} \
\frac{\partial}{\partial x} & \frac{\partial}{\partial y} & \frac{\partial}{\partial z} \
F_{x} & F_{y} & F_{z}
\end{array}\right| \
&=\hat{\mathbf{i}}\left(\frac{\partial F_{z}}{\partial y}-\frac{\partial F_{y}}{\partial z}\right)-\hat{\mathbf{j}}\left(\frac{\partial F_{z}}{\partial x}-\frac{\partial F_{x}}{\partial z}\right)+\hat{\mathbf{k}}\left(\frac{\partial F_{y}}{\partial x}-\frac{\partial F_{x}}{\partial y}\right) .
\end{aligned}
$$
The curl of a vector field is a [[Fields/Fields/Scalar and Vector Field#Vector Field|vector field]].

Please note, as $\nabla$ is a vector operator,then $\nabla \times \mathbf{F} \neq \mathbf{F} \times \nabla$

solenoidal

A vector field with zero divergence is said to be solenoidal.

Formula

Let $\mathrm{G}$ and $\mathrm{H}$ be two differential scalar fields and let $\mathbf{A}$ and $\mathrm{B}$ be two differentiable vector fields. First order differentials

Addition satisfy linear all time

  1. $\nabla(G+H)=\nabla G+\nabla H$
  2. $\nabla \cdot(\mathbf{A}+\mathbf{B})=\nabla \cdot \mathbf{A}+\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B}$
  3. $\nabla \times(\mathbf{A}+\mathbf{B})=\nabla \times \mathbf{A}+\nabla \times \mathbf{B}$

Dot multiplication

  1. $\nabla(G H)=G \nabla H+H \nabla G$.
    Dot just like derivative
  2. $\nabla \cdot(G \mathbf{A})=G \nabla \cdot \mathbf{A}+\mathbf{A} \cdot \nabla G$
  3. $\nabla(\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{B})=\mathbf{A} \times(\nabla \times \mathbf{B})+\mathbf{B} \times(\nabla \times \mathbf{A})+(\mathbf{A} \cdot \nabla) \mathbf{B}+(\mathbf{B} \cdot \nabla) \mathbf{A}$
  4. $\nabla \cdot(\mathbf{A} \times \mathbf{B})=(\nabla \times \mathbf{A}) \cdot \mathbf{B}-(\nabla \times \mathbf{B}) \cdot \mathbf{A}$

Cross multiplication

  1. $\nabla \times(G \mathbf{A})=G \nabla \times \mathbf{A}+(\nabla G) \times \mathbf{A}$
  2. $\nabla \times(\mathbf{A} \times \mathbf{B})=(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B}) \mathbf{A}-(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{A}) \mathbf{B}+(\mathbf{B} \cdot \nabla) \mathbf{A}-(\mathbf{A} \cdot \nabla) \mathbf{B}$

Second order differentials

  1. $\nabla \cdot(\nabla G)=\operatorname{div} \operatorname{grad} G=\nabla^{2} G=\Delta G$
  2. $\nabla \times(\nabla G)=\operatorname{curl} \operatorname{grad} G=0$
  3. $\nabla \cdot(\nabla \times \mathbf{A})=\operatorname{div} \operatorname{curl} \mathbf{A}=0$
  4. $\nabla \times \nabla \times \mathbf{A}=\operatorname{curl} \operatorname{curl} \mathbf{A}=-\nabla^{2} \mathbf{A}+\nabla(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{A})$
    where $\nabla(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{A})=\operatorname{grad} \operatorname{div} \mathbf{A}$

Model

In the case of the second order differential operator formulae, the first identity, i.e., $\nabla \cdot(\nabla G)=$ div grad $G$, is an important operator that occurs in many physical problems. It is called the Laplacian and is denoted by $\nabla^{2} G$ or $\triangle G$ where
$$
\nabla^{2} G=\left(\frac{\partial}{\partial x}, \frac{\partial}{\partial y}, \frac{\partial}{\partial z}\right) \cdot\left(\frac{\partial G}{\partial x}, \frac{\partial G}{\partial y}, \frac{\partial G}{\partial z}\right)=\frac{\partial^{2} G}{\partial x^{2}}+\frac{\partial^{2} G}{\partial y^{2}}+\frac{\partial^{2} G}{\partial z^{2}}
$$
Laplace’s equation is where the Laplacian is equal to zero, i.e., the equation
$$
\nabla^{2} G=0
$$
This equation models:

  1. The distribution of electrostatic potential in a uniform dielectric material or free space where there is no charge.
  2. The distribution of temperature for steady states.
  3. The distribution of an inviscid, irrotational fluid flow.

Poisson’s Equation is a variation of Laplace’s equation where the right hand side is no longer zero, i.e.,
$$
\nabla^{2} G=f(x, y, z)
$$
The equation also occurs widely and models

  1. Unsteady heat flow.
  2. The solution of Gauss’s law for a conservative field.

In other coordinate system

Cartesian system

Let $G=G(x, y, z)$ and $\mathbf{A}=A_{1} \hat{\mathbf{i}}+A_{2} \hat{\mathbf{j}}+A_{3} \hat{\mathbf{k}}$, then
$$
\begin{gathered}
\nabla G=\frac{\partial G}{\partial x} \hat{\mathbf{i}}+\frac{\partial G}{\partial y} \hat{\mathbf{j}}+\frac{\partial G}{\partial z} \hat{\mathbf{k}} \quad \nabla \cdot \mathbf{A}=\frac{\partial A_{1}}{\partial x}+\frac{\partial A_{2}}{\partial y}+\frac{\partial A_{3}}{\partial z} \
\nabla \times \mathbf{A}=\left(\frac{\partial A_{3}}{\partial y}-\frac{\partial A_{2}}{\partial z}\right) \hat{\mathbf{i}}+\left(\frac{\partial A_{1}}{\partial z}-\frac{\partial A_{3}}{\partial x}\right) \hat{\mathbf{j}}+\left(\frac{\partial A_{2}}{\partial x}-\frac{\partial A_{1}}{\partial y}\right) \hat{\mathbf{k}} \
\nabla^{2} G=\frac{\partial^{2} G}{\partial x^{2}}+\frac{\partial^{2} G}{\partial y^{2}}+\frac{\partial^{2} G}{\partial z^{2}}
\end{gathered}
$$

cylindrical system

Let $G=G(r, \theta, z)$ and $\mathbf{A}=A_{r} \hat{\mathbf{r}}+A_{\theta} \hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}+A_{z} \hat{\mathbf{k}}$, then
$$
\begin{gathered}
\nabla G=\frac{\partial G}{\partial r} \hat{\mathbf{r}}+\frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial G}{\partial \theta} \hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}+\frac{\partial G}{\partial z} \hat{\mathbf{k}}, \quad \nabla \cdot \mathbf{A}=\frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial}{\partial r}\left(r A_{r}\right)+\frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial A_{\theta}}{\partial \theta}+\frac{\partial A_{z}}{\partial z} \
\nabla \times \mathbf{A}=\left(\frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial A_{z}}{\partial \theta}-\frac{\partial A_{\theta}}{\partial z}\right) \hat{\mathbf{r}}+\left(\frac{\partial A_{r}}{\partial z}-\frac{\partial A_{z}}{\partial r}\right) \hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}+\left(\frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial}{\partial r}\left(r A_{\theta}\right)-\frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial A_{r}}{\partial \theta}\right) \hat{\mathbf{k}} \
\nabla^{2} G=\frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial}{\partial r}\left(r \frac{\partial G}{\partial r}\right)+\frac{1}{r^{2}} \frac{\partial^{2} G}{\partial \theta^{2}}+\frac{\partial^{2} G}{\partial z^{2}}
\end{gathered}
$$

spherical system

Let $G=G(\rho, \phi, \theta)$ and $\mathbf{A}=A_{\rho} \hat{\boldsymbol{\rho}}+A_{\phi} \hat{\boldsymbol{\phi}}+A_{\theta} \hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$, then
$$
\begin{gathered}
\nabla G=\frac{\partial G}{\partial \rho} \hat{\boldsymbol{\rho}}+\frac{1}{\rho} \frac{\partial G}{\partial \phi} \hat{\boldsymbol{\phi}}+\frac{1}{\rho \sin \phi} \frac{\partial G}{\partial \theta} \hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}} \
\nabla \cdot \mathbf{A}=\frac{1}{\rho^{2}} \frac{\partial}{\partial \rho}\left(\rho^{2} A_{\rho}\right)+\frac{1}{\rho \sin \phi} \frac{\partial}{\partial \phi}\left(\sin \phi A_{\phi}\right)+\frac{1}{\rho \sin \phi} \frac{\partial A_{\theta}}{\partial \theta} \
\nabla \times \mathbf{A}=\frac{1}{\rho \sin \phi}\left(\frac{\partial}{\partial \phi}\left(\sin \phi A_{\theta}\right)-\frac{\partial A_{\phi}}{\partial \theta}\right) \hat{\boldsymbol{\rho}}+\frac{1}{\rho}\left(\frac{1}{\sin \phi} \frac{\partial A_{\rho}}{\partial \theta}-\frac{\partial}{\partial \rho}\left(\rho A_{\theta}\right)\right) \hat{\boldsymbol{\phi}} \
\quad+\frac{1}{\rho}\left(\frac{\partial}{\partial \rho}\left(\rho A_{\phi}\right)-\frac{\partial A_{\rho}}{\partial \phi}\right) \hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}} \
\nabla^{2} G=\frac{1}{\rho^{2}} \frac{\partial}{\partial \rho}\left(\rho^{2} \frac{\partial G}{\partial \rho}\right)+\frac{1}{\rho^{2} \sin \phi} \frac{\partial}{\partial \phi}\left(\sin \phi \frac{\partial G}{\partial \phi}\right)+\frac{1}{\rho^{2} \sin ^{2} \phi} \frac{\partial^{2} G}{\partial \theta^{2}}
\end{gathered}
$$

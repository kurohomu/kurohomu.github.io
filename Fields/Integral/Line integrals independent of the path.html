Line integrals independent of the path

Notion: this chapter only discuss the [[Fields/Fields/Scalar and Vector Field#Vector Field|vector field]]

In many case, the [[Fields/Integral/Curve integral|integral]] depend on the path and end point, we call it path of integral.

However, if the integral satisfy some certain condition, it may be only depend on the end of the point.

Hence, we classify field into two types by the independence.

  1. Conservative fields: [[Fields/Fields/Scalar and Vector Field#Vector Field|vector field]] for which the line integral does not depend on the path $C$ between the points $A$ and $B$ (say) but only on the coordinates $A$ and $B$.
  2. Non-conservative fields: vector fields for which this property does not hold.

#Theorem The Fundamental Theorem of Line Integrals
Suppose that $\mathbf{F}(x, y)=f(x, y) \hat{\mathbf{i}}+g(x, y) \hat{\mathbf{j}}$, where $f$ and $g$ are continuous in some open region containing the points $\left(x_{0}, y_{0}\right)$ and $\left(x_{1}, y_{1}\right)$. If $$\mathbf{F}(x, y)=\nabla \phi(x, y)$$ at each point of this region, then for any piecewise smooth curve $C$ that starts at $\left(x_{0}, y_{0}\right)$, ends at $\left(x_{1}, y_{1}\right)$ and lies entirely in the region$$\int_{C} \mathbf{F} \cdot d \mathbf{r}=\phi\left(x_{1}, y_{1}\right)-\phi\left(x_{0}, y_{0}\right)$$
We shall give the proof for a smooth curve $C$. The proof for a piecewise smooth curve is obtained by considering each piece separately.
We say that the integral is independent of path $C$ or path independent.
The scalar $\phi$ is called a potential function (or potential scalar field) for F in that region.

Proof

Let $C$ be given parametrically by
$$
x=x(t), \quad y=y(t), \quad a \leq t \leq b .
$$
Then the initial and final points of $C$ are
$$
\left(x_{0}, y_{0}\right)=(x(a), y(a)) \quad \text { and } \quad\left(x_{1}, y_{1}\right)=(x(b), y(b)) \text {. }
$$
Since $\mathbf{F}(x, y)=\nabla \phi(x, y)$, it follows that
$$
\mathbf{F}(x, y)=\frac{\partial \phi}{\partial x} \hat{\mathbf{i}}+\frac{\partial \phi}{\partial y} \hat{\mathbf{j}}
$$
which in turn means
$$
\begin{aligned}
\int_{C} \mathbf{F} \cdot d \mathbf{r} &=\int_{C} \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial x} d x+\frac{\partial \phi}{\partial y} d y \
&=\int_{a}^{b}\left(\frac{\partial \phi}{\partial x} \frac{d x}{d t}+\frac{\partial \phi}{\partial y} \frac{d y}{d t}\right) d t \
&=\int_{a}^{b} \frac{d}{d t}[\phi(x(t), y(t))] d t \
&=[\phi(x(t), y(t))]{t=a}^{t=b} \
&=\phi(x(b), y(b))-\phi(x(a), y(a))=\phi\left(x{1}, y_{1}\right)-\phi\left(x_{0}, y_{0}\right)
\end{aligned}
$$

Exist of potential function

#Theorem
Let $\mathbf{F}(x, y)=f(x, y) \hat{\mathbf{i}}+g(x, y) \hat{\mathbf{j}}$, where $f$ and $g$ have continuous first partial derivatives in an [[Complex Analysis/Connected Region#Simple Connected Region|open simply connected region]]. The vector function $\mathbf{F}(x, y)$ is the gradient of some scalar function $\phi(x, y)$ on that region if and only if$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}=\frac{\partial g}{\partial x}$$
at each point of the region.

Proof

Suppose that $\mathbf{F}(x, y)$ is the gradient of some scalar function $\phi(x, y)$, that is,
$$
\mathbf{F}(x, y)=f(x, y) \hat{\mathbf{i}}+g(x, y) \hat{\mathbf{j}}=\frac{\partial \phi}{\partial x} \hat{\mathbf{i}}+\frac{\partial \phi}{\partial y} \hat{\mathbf{j}}=\nabla \phi .
$$
It therefore follows that
$$
f(x, y)=\frac{\partial \phi}{\partial x} \quad \text { and } \quad g(x, y)=\frac{\partial \phi}{\partial y}
$$
which in turn means that
$$
\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}=\frac{\partial^{2} \phi}{\partial y \partial x} \quad \text { and } \quad \frac{\partial g}{\partial x}=\frac{\partial^{2} \phi}{\partial x \partial y} \text {. }
$$
Since $\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}$ and $\frac{\partial g}{\partial x}$ were assumed continous, the scalar potential $\phi$ is twice continuously differentiable, and therefore, its mixed partial derivatives are equal. The result of the latter statement is that
$$
\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}=\frac{\partial g}{\partial x}
$$
This proof is given on the next page only for the case if $\mathbf{F}$ is the gradient of some scalar function.

Step
  1. Rewrite the equation to vector field format and identify the $f,g$
  2. differential $f,g$ by theorem
  3. use fundamental theorem or line integral to find integral

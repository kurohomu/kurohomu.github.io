Curve integral

Just like ordinary integral, curve integral can be trade with the sum product of derivative and function value.
$$
\int_{C} f(x, y) d s=\int_{C} f(s) d s=\lim {\delta s{i} \rightarrow 0} \sum_{i=1}^{N} f\left(s_{i}\right) \delta s_{i}
$$
The only difference is the path can be curve. However, it does not affect the derivative work .

Calculation Trick

Although the essence of curve integral is same to line, there are some dilemma to calculate it. Since the interval of integral is not a number, we should use some trick to convert it to one-dimension.

Substitution

Given the curve integral and the equation of the curve in two dimension. There are two element to be transit-function and derivative. The bridge is the curve equation. See the example in detail. Pay attention to determine the sign according to the increasing $ds$.
$$
d s=\pm \sqrt{d x^{2}+d y^{2}}=\pm\left[\sqrt{1+\left(\frac{d y}{d x}\right)^{2}}\right] d x=\pm\left[\sqrt{\left(\frac{d x}{d y}\right)^{2}+1}\right] d y
$$

Parametric Representation

One important method to solve integral. Similar to substitution, it use another one parameter to representation integral.

Let $C$ be a smooth curve in two-dimensional space given parametrically by the equations
$$
x=x(t), \quad y=y(t), \quad a \leq t \leq b
$$
The line integral of the function $f(x, y)$ along $C$ can be re-written in terms of the parameter $t$. In order to do this we make use of the formula
$$
d s=\pm \sqrt{\left(\frac{d x}{d t}\right)^{2}+\left(\frac{d y}{d t}\right)^{2}} d t
$$
Assuming that $d s$ and $d t$ increase in the same direction, then
$$
\int_{C} f(x, y) d s=\int_{C} f(x(t), y(t)) \sqrt{\left(\frac{d x}{d t}\right)^{2}+\left(\frac{d y}{d t}\right)^{2}} d t
$$

Differential Form

If the curve integral with parametric representation has the form
$$
\int_{C} f(x, y) d x+g(x, y) d y=\int_{C} f(x, y) d x+\int_{C} g(x, y) d y
$$
where
$$
x=x(t), \quad y=y(t), \quad a \leq t \leq b
$$
We could use following formula to solve
$$
d x=\frac{d x}{d t} d t \quad d y=\frac{d y}{d t} d t
$$
This form is relate to vector field case.

Piecewise smooth curves

Consider a curve $C$ formed by finitely many smooth curves $C_{1}, C_{2}, \cdots, C_{n}$ joined end to end. Such a curve is called piecewise smooth.

We define a line integral over a piecewise smooth curve $C$ to be equal to the sum of the integrals over the pieces, i.e.,
$$
\int_{C}=\int_{C_{1}}+\int_{C_{2}}+\cdots+\int_{C_{n}}
$$

Independence of parametrization

#Theorem
If $C$ is a smooth parametric curve, and if a smooth change of parameter is made that does not change the orientation of the curve, then both sets of parametric equations produce the same value for any line integral over $C$.

This theorem tells us for a curve integral the most important things is the path, but not the representation. See more here. See example in notes.

How to memory:

  • We first establish the smooth curve with parameter
  • The process is the smooth change without changing orientation. Any change will be OK.
  • then, the change does not affect the result of the integral

For example, the curve $y=x^2$ cam be representation by both $x(t)=t, y(t)=t^2$ and $x(t)=2t, y(t)=4t^2$, but both of them consequence the same integral.

Reversal of orientation

#Theorem
If $C$ is a smooth parametric curve, and if a smooth change of parameter is made that reverses the orientation of the curve, then for any line integral over $C$ the two sets of parametric equations produce values that are negatives of one another.

A change in the orientation means a change in the direction by which the curve is being traced. If a curve $C$ is traced in a certain direction, then the same curve traced in the opposite direction is often denoted by the symbol $-C$. Therefore, it follows from Theorem that
$$
\int_{-C} f(x, y) d x+g(x, y) d y=-\int_{C} f(x, y) d x+g(x, y) d y
$$

Summary

From the theorem, we know that the curve itself does not have certain direction since it just a curve. However, the parametric representation has the domain integral, which is from $a$ to $b$. Therefore, the parameter can show the curve and additional information.

Vector side

The idea is similar to scalar cases, but the procession.

There are twp element

  1. [[Fields/Fields/Scalar and Vector Field#Vector Field|vector field]]:
    $$\mathbf{F}(x, y)=f(x, y) \hat{\mathbf{i}}+g(x, y) \hat{\mathbf{j}}$$

  2. path:
    $$\mathbf{r}(t)=x(t) \hat{\mathbf{i}}+y(t) \hat{\mathbf{j}}$$

And the two result format

  1. $\int_{C} f(x, y) d x+g(x, y) d y=\int_{C} \mathbf{F}(x, y) \cdot d \mathbf{r}$
  2. $\int_{C} \mathbf{F}(x, y) \cdot d \mathbf{r}=\int_{a}^{b}\left[\mathbf{F}(x(t), y(t)) \cdot \frac{d \mathbf{r}}{d t}\right] d t$

Work

The intuitive is about the integral in coordinate direction. The formula of integral and work is same. This lead us consider the work.

$$W=\int_{a}^{b} \mathbf{F}(x(t), y(t)) \cdot \mathbf{r}^{\prime}(t) d t$$

This formula is derive from $W(t+\Delta t)-W(t) \approx \mathbf{F}(x(t), y(t)) \cdot[\mathbf{r}(t+\Delta t)-\mathbf{r}(t)]$

Step
  1. transfer curve to parameter representation with range
  2. find parameter representation of vector field
  3. solve the derivative of curve
  4. use formula

3-D case

All of the ideas presented in the previous sections for line integration in two dimensions can be extended in a straightforward manner to three dimensions. Here we consider the differential form and the line integration of vector functions in three dimensions.
[[Fields/Fields/Scalar and Vector Field#Scalar Field|Scalar field]]:
$$\begin{aligned} & \int_{C} f(x, y, z) d x+g(x, y, z) d y+h(x, y, z) d z \=& \int_{C} f(x, y, z) d x+\int_{C} g(x, y, z) d y+\int_{C} h(x, y, z) d z \end{aligned}$$
[[Fields/Fields/Scalar and Vector Field#Vector Field|vector field]]:
$$\int_{C} \mathbf{F}(x, y, z) \cdot d \mathbf{r}=\int_{a}^{b}\left(\mathbf{F}(x(t), y(t), z(t)) \cdot \frac{d \mathbf{r}}{d t}\right) d t$$

Step to solve equation
  1. use fundamental assume to process equation
  2. find derivative of curve
  3. use parameter representation
  4. solve equation
